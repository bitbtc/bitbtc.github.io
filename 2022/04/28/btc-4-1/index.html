Bitcoin Long-Term Fundamental Trends Are Strengthening

发表于2022-04-28|更新于2022-04-28|cryptocurrency
|阅读量:

Yields, implied volatility and trading volumes in the bitcoin derivatives market continue to shrink, leading to a decline in total capital inflows. On-chain trading volumes have also been subdued, but more constructive medium- to long-term fundamental trends are starting to develop.

Volatility and trading volume continue to shrink across the bitcoin market, with prices remaining within a consolidation range of $38,000 to $42,000. The market price fell slightly again this week, retreating from a high of $42,893 and dropping to a one-week low of $38,728.

The market has been trading within an increasingly narrow price range for nearly three months, resulting in historically low yields on cash arbitrage trades in the futures market, while trading volumes continue to decline. Implied volatility priced in the options market also fell below 60% this week, well below the 80%+ volatility seen for most of 2021. In addition, trading volumes on the chain remain subdued despite the growing trend of high-value ($10 million+) trades and declining macro volumes associated with FX inflows and outflows.

In this issue, we will focus on some of the megatrends in the developing Bitcoin market, including.

Shrinking trading volumes in the futures market, low implied option volatility and rolling basis yields that continue to be below 3% in the futures market. All of these have led to capital flows out of the bitcoin market as investors seek higher returns elsewhere.
The dominance of the perpetual futures market continues to improve as these instruments have clearly become the preferred source of leverage.
However, on-chain settlement volumes continue to decline as the dominance of larger transactions ($10M+) grows.
The cyclical difference between exchange-related inflows/outflows and total transaction volumes. This is accompanied by a potential shift in network utilization and provides a potentially constructive reversal in the underlying implied valuation of Bitcoin.


Perpetual Futures Dominance on the Rise

The bitcoin derivatives market has matured rapidly over the past five years or so. It has shifted from a small portion of spot trading in 2017 to now representing the primary venue for market pricing. Currently, futures trading volume is several times higher than that of the spot market.

Despite this, total futures trading volume has been in massive decline since January 2021. In the first half of 2021, daily trading volumes typically ranged from $70 billion to $80 billion; however, in the current market, futures volumes are down more than 59% to approximately $30.7 billion/day. During October-November 2021, there was little to no rally, despite the fact that prices set new all-time highs.

Open interest in the futures market is also significantly lower than at the peak of the bull market, currently hovering around $15 billion with a 2:1 ratio between perpetual and calendar expiration futures. current open interest is similar to levels seen during the May-September 2021 period, but is still 36.8% below the peaks of over $22.5 billion set in April and November.

Perpetual futures are increasingly becoming the instrument of choice for trading, a trend that can be clearly seen in the strength chart below:

Perpetual swap volume, represented in blue, dominates relative to total futures volume, currently showing a significant 92.4% advantage. This is up from a 75% dominance in December 2020 as the market breaks through the highest value ever reached in the last cycle of $20,000.
Pink represents the same dominant concept, but for open futures volume, which has risen from 50% dominance to over 66% dominance since December 2020.
In many ways, this trend is predictable and may be the result of several key factors.

Perpetual swaps are priced more closely to spot indices, making it easier and more intuitive for traders to manage positions and leverage.
The lower storage and delivery costs associated with digital assets compared to physical commodities offset many of the benefits of calendar futures. Calendar futures give a useful tool for hedging risk and pricing future production and delivery costs for physical commodities, but for Bitcoin, these costs are close to zero.

This trend is also reflected in the futures leverage ratio, which remains in a structural upward trend. This reflects the market’s increasing tendency to preferentially allocate capital to the perpetual swap market over calendar futures. Currently, the open interest in perpetual swaps is equivalent to 1.3% of Bitcoin’s market capitalization, which is approaching an all-time high.

However, it is worth noting that total leverage across all futures markets has actually declined over the past two weeks (from 2.1% in early April to 1.9% now). Thus, despite the relatively high open interest in perpetual swaps, the total capital and leverage shift in calendar maturity futures is much larger, resulting in a net decrease in overall leverage.

Yield and volatility continue to compress

While leverage in perpetual swaps continues to rise, leverage in all futures markets is declining, suggesting that a significant amount of capital is actually leaving the bitcoin market. This trend is further supported by the aforementioned decline in trading volume.

If we look at perpetual swap financing rates, we can see that for most of 2022, available yields were very low and with little directional bias. This is in contrast to the ultra-bullish long-term speculation in the first half of 2021 and from August to November, and the extreme bearish period from May to July 2021.

If we discount the perpetual funding rate to an adult rate and compare it to the three-month rolling benchmark available in calendar futures, we can see the possible reasons for capital outflows from the bitcoin market.

Yields in the futures market have compressed to just above 3.0%, which is barely above the yield on the 10-year U.S. Treasury (2.9%) and also significantly below the recent U.S. CPI inflation rate of 8.5%. The declining trading rates and falling open interest are likely signs of capital flowing out of bitcoin derivatives towards higher yielding and potentially lower perceived risk opportunities.

We can also see that implied volatility in the options market is at an all-time low, falling below 60% in the last few weeks. Implied volatility has rarely reached such low levels over the past year, most of which is within the current consolidation range that encompasses almost the entire year-to-date price action.

Due to the low implied volatility in the options market, options short sellers are in a similarly low return position as spot and arbitrage futures traders.

We can also see a general shift in the risk management of put option ratios associated with option trading volume (blue) and open interest (pink). Demand for call options dominates for most of 2021 until September, when bullish sentiment appears to fade. As 2022 begins, more bearish sentiment takes hold, demand to hedge downside risk increases, and a clear market preference for puts emerges.

Divergence in on-chain trading volume

Transitioning from the derivatives market to the on-chain settlement market, we can see a similar trend of declining total trading volume. Bitcoin’s network is currently settling between $5.5 billion and $7 billion per day, down approximately 40% from the bull market peak of $9.5 billion to $11 billion in daily settlement value.

However, settlement volumes remain significantly higher than the $2 billion/day seen in 2019-2020, suggesting a net increase in network utilization.

There also appears to be a structural shift in the breakdown of transaction sizes, particularly after October 2020, with the chart below showing the relative breakdown of transaction volumes by dollar value, with the explosive growth in the dominance of transaction sizes above $10,000,000 (dark green) very evident. Prior to October 2020, these large transactions accounted for only 10% of total transactions on significant days, but now consistently dominate at 40%.

Note that these charts use our entity-adjusted data, which filters out non-economic transactions such as internal exchange wallet management and entity self-spending. This continued dominance of large transactions likely reflects the true growth in value settlement by institutional-sized investment/trading entities, custodians, and high-net-worth individuals.

The volume of transfers to and from exchanges has always represented a significant portion of the overall transaction flow, so it is important to assess this on a case-by-case basis. The chart below shows the total transaction volume (blue), adjusted for entities, compared to USD-denominated FX inflows (green) and outflows (red).

First, we can see that inflows and outflows are typically very similar in size, and trajectories often overlap each other, at least on this scale. Currently, total FX flows are about $2.1 billion per day, with a slight edge in outflows ($1.1 billion/day) compared to inflows of $1 billion/day.

We can then use the ratio between total FX flows (inflows + outflows) and total transfers to try to see the cyclical patterns associated with FX dominance.

In particular, on-chain volumes associated with FX inflows/outflows have typically increased during more speculative and bullish bull periods (e.g., the 2016-2017 bull market) since 2016, as well as the period from July 2019 to May 2021.
Conversely, the share of exchange-related activity relative to total value settlement typically declines in the later stages of bear markets, such as 2018-19, and since May 2021.
Currently, exchanges account for about 32% of all settlement value, which is a relatively low share. This may indicate a gradual shift from pure speculation to more fundamental demand drivers such as OTC trading, accumulation of firm bitcoin holders and escrow multi-signature setups.

Finally, we can take these observations of total transaction volume and model them as a fundamental pricing model using the approach first proposed by Willy Woo. The NVT price model takes the 2-year median of the NVT ratio and multiplies it by the current transaction volume. The resulting model therefore creates an implied valuation based on the current level of usage of bitcoin used for value settlement.

The 28-day (green) and 90-day (pink) cycles were used to create the fast and slow signals, respectively. Both models currently value Bitcoin between $32,500 (90 days) and $36,100, both of which are beginning to bottom out and could rebound to a reversal. Of note is the recent positive momentum crossover, with the 28-day cycle breaking above the 90-day cycle much faster.

Historically, such crossovers are constructive medium- to long-term signals. However, as shown in blue on the chart below, these signals do need time to confirm before the positive momentum is in play. Greater on-chain value settlement will trigger an increase in both models, which suggests stronger underlying fundamentals (and vice versa).

Summary and Conclusion

The Bitcoin derivatives market has matured significantly in recent years and its underlying structure has evolved. We have seen a clear shift in preferred instruments from calendar expiration futures to perpetual contract markets, a shift that is to be expected given the ease of interpretation of digital asset prices and the lower cost of storing and delivering digital assets.

Over the past 12 months, we have seen trading volumes, implied volatility and available cash and arbitrage yields shrink to historic lows, which appears to have prompted some capital to leave the bitcoin space in search of higher returns. With cash and arbitrage yields continuing to fall below 3.0% and overall inflation reaching 8.5%, the likelihood of this occurring will grow.

Interestingly, despite similarly subdued on-chain settlement volumes, underlying fundamentals are shaping up to be a growing (but early) trend. Transactions over $10 million have maintained a 40% dominance since the end of 2020, while the dominance of speculative FX inflows and outflows typically associated with bull markets appears to be declining.

The implied valuations of NVT price models are still in the low to mid $30,000 range, which does suggest that the bulls have a lot of work to do. However, these models may be starting to bottom out, which is a development worth watching closely in the coming weeks.
